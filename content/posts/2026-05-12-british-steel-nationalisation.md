Prime Minister Keir Starmer confirmed on Tuesday that the UK government will move forward with plans to nationalise British Steel, a decision that marks one of the most significant state interventions in British industry in decades.

Speaking at a press conference in Westminster, Starmer argued that government ownership is necessary to secure the future of the struggling steelmaker, which employs thousands of workers across the UK. "British Steel is a strategic national asset. Allowing it to collapse or fall into foreign hands without safeguards would be a failure of leadership," he said.

The announcement comes after months of uncertainty over the company's financial viability. British Steel has faced mounting pressures from high energy costs, aging infrastructure, and global market oversupply, particularly from China. The government has already provided multiple bailout packages to keep the company operational.

Critics, including opposition Conservative MPs and some business groups, have warned that nationalisation will burden taxpayers and stifle private investment. Shadow Business Secretary Claire Coutinho called the plan "a reckless leap backwards" that would "undo decades of market reform."

However, unions broadly welcomed the move. Community Union general secretary Roy Rickhuss said: "This is a landmark moment. Workers at British Steel have been living under a cloud of uncertainty for too long. Public ownership offers a path to sustainable investment and job security."

The government has not yet disclosed the full cost of the nationalisation or the compensation terms for current owners. A detailed white paper is expected within the next 30 days, outlining the transition timeline and governance structure of the newly nationalised entity.
