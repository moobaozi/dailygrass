Prime Minister Keir Starmer is facing the most serious challenge to his leadership yet, as a growing faction of Labour MPs openly calls for his resignation. The internal rebellion threatens to destabilise a government already grappling with a narrow parliamentary majority and a series of policy setbacks.

According to multiple reports from Westminster, at least two dozen Labour MPs have either publicly or privately indicated that they believe Starmer should step aside before the next general election. Discontent has been simmering for months, fueled by stagnant polling numbers, internal divisions over economic policy, and criticism of the government's handling of public services.

"Colleagues are losing confidence. The mood in the party is bleak," one Labour MP told the *Financial Times*, speaking on condition of anonymity. "We need a fresh start if we're going to win the next election."

The unrest has been exacerbated by a string of by-election losses and internal surveys showing the party trailing the Conservatives in key marginal seats. Critics within the party argue that Starmer has failed to articulate a compelling vision for the country and has governed too cautiously.

Downing Street has sought to downplay the revolt. A spokesperson for the Prime Minister said: "The PM is focused on delivering for the British people, not on Westminster gossip. He retains the support of the parliamentary party."

However, the scale of the discontent is difficult to dismiss. A meeting of the Parliamentary Labour Party scheduled for later this week is expected to be tense, with some MPs pushing for a formal vote of confidence in the leader.

Political analysts note that removing a sitting prime minister would be procedurally complex and politically risky. But with the party divided and the next election looming, pressure on Starmer shows no signs of abating.
